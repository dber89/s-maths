What is a Percentage?

The word per cent comes from Latin: per centum — meaning out of one hundred. The symbol % is a shorthand for "÷ 100".

The % Symbol

%
per cent · out of 100 · ÷ 100

Think of the % symbol itself as a clue: the two little circles are like zeros, and the dividing line says "out of" — so % literally pictures 0/0 with a line, hinting at hundredths.

When we write 35%, we mean 35 out of every 100. If there were 100 children in a school and 35 had packed lunch, then 35% have packed lunch.

Real-life examples

100% of something is all of it. If you eat 100% of your dinner, you've eaten every last bite.

50% is half — exactly 50 out of 100.

0% means none at all.

Percentages can go above 100% — if a shop sells 150% more than last year, it sold half as much again on top of everything it sold before.

The one rule to burn into memory: % always means "out of 100". Every calculation you do with percentages comes back to this one idea.
Worked example — reading % in context

"In a class of 100 pupils, 63 passed the test. What percentage passed?"

1
63 out of 100 passed.
2
Percentage = the number out of 100 = 63%.

(When the total is already 100, the percentage equals the count directly. It gets more interesting when the total isn't 100 — that's a later lesson.)

Writing % as Fractions & Decimals

A percentage, a fraction, and a decimal are three different ways of writing the same thing. Knowing how to move between them quickly is one of the most tested 11+ skills.

Percentage → Fraction

Because % means "out of 100", any percentage converts straight to a fraction with denominator 100:

47% = 47/100

You may then be able to simplify. For instance:

20% = 20/100 = 1/5

Divide top and bottom by the same number (the HCF) to simplify. 20 and 100 both divide by 20, giving 1/5.

Worked example — % to fraction

"Write 75% as a fraction in its simplest form."

1
Write over 100: 75/100
2
Find the HCF of 75 and 100. Both divide by 25.
3
75 ÷ 25 = 3, 100 ÷ 25 = 4 → answer: 3/4

Percentage → Decimal

Dividing by 100 moves the decimal point two places to the left:

35% → 0.35
7% → 0.07
100% → 1.00
Common mistake

7% is 0.07, not 0.7. You need two places to the left — 7 becomes 07 with a leading zero, giving 0.07. Draw a column table if unsure.

Worked example — % to decimal

"Write 3% as a decimal."

1
3% means 3 ÷ 100
2
Move the point two places left: 3.00 → 0.03
3
Answer: 0.03

Decimal → Percentage

Go the other way: multiply by 100 (move point two places right):

0.45 → 45%
0.08 → 8%
1.25 → 125%
Worked example — decimal to %

"Write 0.6 as a percentage."

1
Multiply by 100: 0.6 × 100 = 60
2
Answer: 60%

How to derive any equivalence you don't know

For any fraction, convert to a decimal by dividing the top by the bottom, then multiply by 100 for the percentage.

Example — 3/8
1
Divide: 3 ÷ 8 = 0.375
2
× 100: 0.375 × 100 = 37.5%

Or use the "make denominator 100" shortcut when the denominator divides into 100:

Example — 7/20
1
20 × 5 = 100, so multiply top and bottom by 5.
2
7/20 = 35/100 = 35%

Comparing fractions using %

Percentages make it easy to compare fractions that look incomparable:

"Which is larger: 3/8 or 2/5?"

3/8 = 37.5% · 2/5 = 40%

So 2/5 is larger. This technique comes up regularly in 11+ papers.

Calculating % of an Amount

This is the most commonly examined percentage skill. There are several methods — learn the one that feels fastest, but understand all of them.

Method 1 — divide then multiply (always works)

To find P% of N:

Step 1

Divide N by 100 to find 1%

Step 2

Multiply your answer by P

Find 35% of 240
1
1% of 240 = 240 ÷ 100 = 2.4
2
35% = 2.4 × 35 = 84

Method 2 — build from 10% (often quickest)

Find 10% by dividing by 10, then build the percentage you need from multiples and halves:

Find 35% of 240
1
10% of 240 = 240 ÷ 10 = 24
2
30% = 24 × 3 = 72
3
5% = half of 10% = 24 ÷ 2 = 12
4
35% = 30% + 5% = 72 + 12 = 84 ✓

Method 3 — fraction shortcut (for common %)

For percentages you know as simple fractions, convert and use the fraction directly:

Find 25% of 360
1
25% = 1/4
2
360 ÷ 4 = 90
Find 33⅓% of 270
1
33⅓% = 1/3
2
270 ÷ 3 = 90
Which method to use? For a percentage like 17% or 43%, use Method 1 or 2 — they always work. For 25%, 50%, 75%, 33⅓%, use Method 3 — it's instant.
Watch out

"Find 15% of 80" — students often find 10% = 8, then forget to add 5%. 15% = 10% + 5% = 8 + 4 = 12, not 8.

Multi-step % problems

At City of London and Westminster level, percentages often appear inside a larger problem. Here is a typical style:

Exam-style question

"A coat costs £180. It is reduced by 30% in a sale. What is the sale price?"

1
Find 30% of £180:
10% = £18  ·  30% = £54
2
Subtract the reduction: £180 − £54 = £126
3
Sale price = £126

Alternative: 100% − 30% = 70%, so find 70% of £180 = £126 directly. Quicker!

Exam-style — finding the original

"After a 20% increase, a price is £60. What was the original price?"

1
£60 represents 120% (original + 20%).
2
1% = £60 ÷ 120 = £0.50
3
100% = £0.50 × 100 = £50

This reverse-percentage type appears at top school level — it's harder but very learnable.
