Lesson 01 · Unit 1: Conversions
Metric Unit Conversions
Converting between km, m, cm, mm · kg, g · litres, ml
The Golden Rule
Smaller unit → Multiply  |  Larger unit → Divide km →(×1000)→ m →(×100)→ cm →(×10)→ mm kg →(×1000)→ g      litres →(×1000)→ ml
The metric length chain — move right to multiply, left to divide
Example A

Convert 3.45 km to metres.

3.45 × 1,000 = 3,450 m
Example B

Convert 2,750 g to kg.

2,750 ÷ 1,000 = 2.75 kg
Moving the decimal point: ×10 → 1 place right · ×100 → 2 places · ×1000 → 3 places

Lesson 02 · Unit 1: Conversions
Metric & Imperial Units
Approximate equivalences between metric and common imperial units
Must-know equivalences
In the 11+, you'll be given the conversion — you just decide × or ÷
Imperial≈MetricTip
1 mile≈1.6 km5 miles = 8 km (key ratio)
1 inch≈2.54 cmRoughly 2.5 cm
1 foot≈30 cm12 inches = 1 foot
1 pound (lb)≈454 g1 kg ≈ 2.2 lbs
1 pint≈568 ml1 litre ≈ 1.75 pints
1 gallon≈4.546 l≈ 4.5 l in exams
Example A

A baby weighs 8 pounds. Approximately how many kg? (1 kg ≈ 2.2 lbs)

8 ÷ 2.2 ≈ 3.6 kg
Example B

A road sign says 30 miles. Convert to km. (5 miles = 8 km)

30 ÷ 5 × 8 = 6 × 8 = 48 km
Going from a smaller imperial unit to metric usually means multiplying. Going from metric to imperial usually means dividing. Draw a quick number line if unsure!

Lesson 03 · Unit 1: Conversions
Time Problems
Converting between seconds, minutes, hours, days — and solving word problems
Core facts to know cold:
60 sec = 1 min  ·  60 min = 1 hr  ·  24 hr = 1 day  ·  7 days = 1 week  ·  365 days = 1 year pm → 24-hr: add 12    e.g. 3:45 pm = 15:45
Example A — Converting to minutes

A film lasts 2 hours 35 minutes. How many minutes total?

2 × 60 + 35 = 120 + 35 = 155 minutes
Example B — Finding journey time

A train leaves at 09:47 and arrives at 12:23. How long is the journey?

09:47 → 10:00 = 13 min · 10:00 → 12:00 = 2 hr · 12:00 → 12:23 = 23 min → Total: 2 hr 36 min
For journey times, count up in chunks: minutes to the next hour, then full hours, then remaining minutes. Never subtract directly — you'll make errors!

Lesson 04 · Unit 1: Conversions
Decimals & Scaling
Map scales, model scales, and decimal measurement problems
Map / Model scales: A scale of 1:25,000 means every 1 cm on the map = 25,000 cm in real life Real distance = map distance × scale number
Map scale example: 6 cm at 1:50,000 = 300,000 cm = 3 km in real life
Example — Model car

A model car is built at scale 1:20. The real car is 3.6 m long. How long is the model in cm?

3.6 m = 360 cm · 360 ÷ 20 = 18 cm
Always convert to the same units first before applying the scale. Watch out for m vs cm!

Lesson 05 · Unit 1: Conversions
Standard Units to 3 d.p.
Reading, writing and converting with decimals up to 3 decimal places
3 decimal places = thousandths
1.075 kg = 1 kg and 75 g  ·  NOT 1 kg 750 g!
kg + g: g ÷ 1000 → decimal kg km + m: m ÷ 1000 → decimal km
The zero in the hundredths column is crucial — 3.045 ≠ 3.45
Example A

Write 4 km 60 m as a decimal number of km.

60 ÷ 1,000 = 0.060 = 0.06 → Answer: 4.060 km (or 4.06 km)
Example B

Write 2.037 litres in ml.

2.037 × 1,000 = 2,037 ml
Zeros between the decimal point and a digit matter! 1.045 ≠ 1.45. Write them carefully.

Lesson 06 · Unit 1: Conversions
Miles & Kilometres
The 5:8 ratio — the most tested conversion in the 11+
The ratio to memorise: 5 miles = 8 km miles → km: ÷ 5 then × 8 km → miles: ÷ 8 then × 5
5 miles is always equal to 8 km — the bars show the ratio
Example A — Miles to km

Convert 40 miles to km.

40 ÷ 5 = 8  ·  8 × 8 = 64 km
Example B — km to miles

Convert 56 km to miles.

56 ÷ 8 = 7  ·  7 × 5 = 35 miles
Divide first — it's always easier to work with smaller numbers before multiplying back up.

Lesson 07 · Unit 2: Perimeter & Area
Perimeter of Composite Shapes
L-shapes, T-shapes and other rectilinear figures
Perimeter = total distance around the outside
For composite shapes: label ALL sides first, find missing ones using opposite-side relationships, then add everything up. Missing side = overall length − known portion
Always label all 6 sides before adding — don't skip the missing ones!
Finding a missing side

An L-shape is 12 cm wide overall. The top part is 7 cm wide. What is the bottom step width?

12 − 7 = 5 cm (opposite sides of rectilinear shapes always reconcile)
Rectilinear = only right angles. The sum of the right-going sides equals the sum of the left-going sides. Use this to find unknowns!

Lesson 08 · Unit 2: Perimeter & Area
Area of Rectangles & Squares
A = l × w and A = s² — including working backwards
Rectangle: A = length × width A = l × w Square: A = side² A = s × s Area is always in square units: cm², m², km²
Example — Working backwards (11+ favourite!)

A rectangle has area 48 cm² and length 8 cm. Find the width.

width = 48 ÷ 8 = 6 cm
If they give you the area and one side, always divide. Area ÷ known side = unknown side.

Lesson 09 · Unit 2: Perimeter & Area
Estimating Area of Irregular Shapes
Grid counting method for curves and irregular outlines
Grid method (3 steps):
1. Count all complete squares inside
2. Count squares more than half inside — add these
3. Ignore squares less than half inside Estimated area ≈ complete + mostly-inside squares
Green = count fully · Amber = count if more than half inside · White = ignore
Always show your working: write "Complete squares: X, Partial squares: Y, Total ≈ X+Y cm²". Examiners want to see method!

Estimating Volume & Capacity
1 ml = 1 cm³ — the link between capacity and volume
Key link: 1 ml = 1 cm³  ·  1 litre = 1,000 cm³ Volume (cm³) = Capacity (ml) Reference: teaspoon ≈ 5 ml · mug ≈ 250 ml · bottle ≈ 500 ml · carton ≈ 1,000 ml

Lesson 11 · Unit 2: Perimeter & Area
Same Area, Different Perimeter
A classic 11+ trap — and how to beat it
Two shapes can have the same area but completely different perimeters.
The squarer the shape → the smaller the perimeter for a given area.
The more stretched → the larger the perimeter.
Same area (24 cm²), very different perimeters. The squarish shape always wins on perimeter!
The square is always the most efficient rectangle — it has the smallest perimeter for any given area. This comes up in "fencing" questions!

Lesson 13 · Unit 2: Area
Area of Triangles
½ × base × height — and the perpendicular height trap
Area of a triangle = ½ × base × perpendicular height A = ½ × b × h ⚠️ The height MUST be perpendicular (90°) to the base — never the slant side!
Left: right-angled triangle (h is a side). Right: scalene — h is the dashed vertical, NOT the slant side!
Working backwards

Area = 30 cm², height = 6 cm. Find the base.

30 = ½ × b × 6 → 30 = 3b → b = 10 cm
Memory trick: a triangle is always exactly half the rectangle that fits around it. Sketch the rectangle if you're confused!

Lesson 14 · Unit 2: Area
Area of Parallelograms
base × perpendicular height — and why the slant side is a red herring
Area of a parallelogram = base × perpendicular height A = b × h ⚠️ NOT base × slant side. The slant side is always a distractor in exam questions!
The dashed red line is the perpendicular height — always at 90° to the base
Why does this formula work? Slice the triangle off one end and slide it to the other — you get a rectangle with the same base and height. Rectangle area = b × h!

Lesson 15 · Unit 3: Volume
Volume of Cubes & Cuboids
V = l × w × h and V = s³ — the most important 3D formula
Cuboid: V = length × width × height V = l × w × h Cube: V = side³ V = s × s × s Volume in cm³ · Remember: 1 cm³ = 1 ml · 1,000 cm³ = 1 litre
Cuboid volume — multiply all three dimensions together
Working backwards

V = 120 cm³, l = 6 cm, w = 4 cm. Find the height.

h = 120 ÷ (6 × 4) = 120 ÷ 24 = 5 cm
For working backwards: find the area of the base first (l × w), then divide the volume by that area to get the height.
