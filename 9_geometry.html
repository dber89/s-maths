Topic 1 of 16 · Properties of shape
3D Shapes from 2D Views
Identify 3D shapes from 2D representations
Plan and elevation views
Plan view
The view from directly above — a bird's eye view of the shape.
Front elevation
The view from directly in front of the shape.
Side elevation
The view from directly to the side of the shape.
Isometric drawing
A 3D shape drawn on a triangular dot grid. Vertical edges stay vertical; depth is shown at 30° to the horizontal.
Common shapes and their 2D views
Cube
All three views — plan, front, side — are identical squares.
Cuboid
All views are rectangles, but possibly different sizes.
Cylinder
Plan view = circle. Front elevation = rectangle.
Cone
Plan view = circle. Front elevation = triangle.
Square-based pyramid
Plan = square with diagonals to centre. Front elevation = triangle.
Triangular prism
Front elevation = rectangle. End elevation = triangle.
Worked examples

Circular plan view + rectangular front elevation → Cylinder.

Square plan view + triangular front elevation → Square-based pyramid.

All three views are identical squares → Cube.

Topic 2 of 16 · Properties of shape
Estimating and Comparing Angles
Estimate and compare acute, obtuse and reflex angles
Angle types
Acute
Greater than 0° and less than 90°. Sharp, narrow.
Right angle
Exactly 90°. Marked with a small square.
Obtuse
Greater than 90° and less than 180°.
Straight
Exactly 180°. A flat line.
Reflex
Greater than 180° and less than 360°.
Estimating without a protractor
Use 90° as anchor
Ask: is this angle less than, equal to, or greater than a right angle?
45° benchmark
Halfway between 0° and 90°.
135° benchmark
Halfway between 90° and 180°.
Reflex check
If it opens past a straight line, it is reflex. Non-reflex partner = 360° minus the angle.

Acute: 0°–90° · Right: 90° · Obtuse: 90°–180°

Straight: 180° · Reflex: 180°–360°

Worked examples

Angle slightly wider than a right angle, less than a straight line → obtuse.

Angle of 250° → reflex. Non-reflex partner = 360° − 250° = 110° (obtuse).

One-third of a right angle → approximately 30° (acute).

Topic 3 of 16 · Properties of shape
Drawing and Measuring Angles
Draw given angles and measure them in degrees
Measuring with a protractor
Step 1
Place the centre hole exactly on the vertex — the corner point of the angle.
Step 2
Line the baseline along one arm of the angle.
Step 3
Read from 0° on the correct scale to where the other arm crosses.
Sense check
If the angle looks acute, your reading must be under 90°. If not, subtract from 180°.
Reflex angles
Measure the non-reflex part first, then subtract from 360°.
Drawing an angle
Step 1
Draw a baseline and mark the vertex.
Step 2
Place protractor centre on vertex; line baseline to 0°.
Step 3
Mark a dot at the required degree on the correct scale.
Step 4
Draw a line from the vertex through the dot. Label the angle.
Worked examples

Read 130° but angle looks acute → wrong scale. Correct = 180° − 130° = 50°.

Reflex 300°: measure non-reflex (60°) first, then label the 300° region.

Drawing 135°: count to 135° on the outer scale, mark dot, draw line.

Topic 4 of 16 · Properties of shape
Angle Rules: Lines, Points and 90°
Use facts about angles at a point, on a straight line and multiples of 90°
Core rules
Straight line
Angles on a straight line add up to 180°.
e.g. 65° + x = 180° → x = 115°
Around a point
Angles around a point add up to 360°.
e.g. 120° + 95° + x = 360° → x = 145°
Vertically opposite
Equal — formed when two straight lines cross.
In a right angle
Angles in a right angle add up to 90°.
Complementary
Two angles that add to 90°.
Supplementary
Two angles that add to 180°.

Straight line: 180° · Around a point: 360°

Right angle: 90° · Vertically opposite: equal

Worked examples

Two angles on a line in ratio 2:1 → 180°÷3 = 60°; angles are 120° and 60°.

Angles at point: 120° + 95° + x = 360° → x = 145°.

Lines cross, one angle = 73° → opposite = 73°; adjacent = 107°.

Topic 5 of 16 · Properties of shape
Rectangle Properties
Use rectangle properties to find missing lengths and angles
Properties of a rectangle
Opposite sides equal
Both long sides equal; both short sides equal.
All angles 90°
Every corner is a right angle.
Diagonals equal
Both diagonals have the same length.
Diagonals bisect
Each diagonal cuts the other exactly in half.
Finding missing values
Missing length
Half perimeter = length + width. Missing side = half perimeter − known side.
Missing angle
Diagonal creates a right-angled triangle inside. One angle is 90°; the other two sum to 90°.

Half perimeter = length + width

Missing side = half perimeter − known side

Diagonal triangle: 90° + angle₁ + angle₂ = 180°

Worked examples

Perimeter 40 cm, length 14 cm → half perim = 20; width = 20 − 14 = 6 cm.

Diagonal makes 34° with long side → angle with short side = 90° − 34° = 56°.

Diagonals each 10 cm → centre to corner = 10 ÷ 2 = 5 cm.

Topic 6 of 16 · Properties of shape
Regular and Irregular Polygons
Distinguish between regular and irregular polygons
The key distinction
Regular polygon
ALL sides equal AND all angles equal.
e.g. equilateral triangle, square, regular pentagon/hexagon.
Irregular polygon
Sides or angles NOT all equal.
A rectangle has equal angles (90°) but unequal sides — it is irregular.
Interior angles of regular polygons
Sum of interior angles
(n − 2) × 180°
Each interior angle
(n − 2) × 180° ÷ n
Each exterior angle
360° ÷ n
Pentagon (5)
Each angle = 108°. Sum = 540°.
Hexagon (6)
Each angle = 120°. Sum = 720°.
Octagon (8)
Each angle = 135°. Sum = 1080°.

Each interior angle = (n − 2) × 180° ÷ n

Each exterior angle = 360° ÷ n

Interior + exterior = 180°

Worked examples

Exterior angle = 45° → sides = 360° ÷ 45° = 8 (octagon).

Regular hexagon: (6−2)×180° ÷ 6 = 720° ÷ 6 = 120° each.

Rectangle: equal angles (all 90°) but sides NOT all equal → irregular.

Topic 7 of 16 · Properties of shape
Drawing 2D Shapes Accurately
Draw 2D shapes using given dimensions and angles
Three triangle construction methods
SAS — Side-Angle-Side
Draw one side, measure the angle at one end, draw the second side to length, join.
SSS — Side-Side-Side
Draw the base, use compasses to draw arcs from each end; arcs cross at the third vertex.
ASA — Angle-Side-Angle
Draw the base, measure angles at each end, extend lines until they meet.
General rules
Baseline first
Start with the longest given side.
Sharp pencil
Use HB or H for accuracy.
Ruler for lengths
Measure in millimetres.
Verify after drawing
Measure all sides and angles to check.
Worked examples

6 cm, 8 cm, included angle 50° → SAS (two sides + angle between them).

All three sides given → SSS (compasses for each arc).

Two angles + side between → ASA.

Topic 8 of 16 · Properties of shape
3D Shapes and Nets
Recognise, describe and build 3D shapes, including making nets
What is a net?
Definition
A flat 2D shape that folds along its edges to make a 3D solid.
Cube
6 identical squares. Exactly 11 valid nets.
Cuboid
6 rectangles in 3 matching pairs.
Triangular prism
2 congruent triangles + 3 rectangles.
Square pyramid
1 square + 4 triangles attached to each side.
Cylinder
2 circles + 1 rectangle (width = circumference = 2πr).
Checking a valid net
Count faces
Does the net have the correct number of faces?
Check connections
Every face must share a complete edge with at least one other.
Mental fold test
Will it fold without gaps or overlaps?

A cube has exactly 11 valid nets

Cylinder: rectangle width = 2πr (the circumference)

Worked examples

1 square + 4 triangles attached to its sides → square-based pyramid.

2 circles + 1 rectangle (width = circumference) → cylinder.

Exactly 11 valid nets for a cube — not all 6-square arrangements fold correctly.

Topic 9 of 16 · Properties of shape
Classifying Geometric Shapes
Compare and classify geometric shapes by properties and size
Triangles — by sides
Equilateral
3 equal sides. All angles 60°. 3 lines of symmetry.
Isosceles
2 equal sides. Base angles equal. 1 line of symmetry.
Scalene
No equal sides or angles. No lines of symmetry.
Triangles — by angles
Acute
All angles less than 90°.
Right-angled
One angle exactly 90°.
Obtuse
One angle greater than 90°.
Quadrilateral hierarchy
Square
4 equal sides, 4 right angles. Special rectangle and rhombus.
Rectangle
4 right angles, opposite sides equal. Diagonals equal.
Parallelogram
2 pairs of parallel sides; opposite sides and angles equal.
Rhombus
4 equal sides; diagonals bisect at 90°.
Trapezium
Exactly 1 pair of parallel sides.
Kite
2 pairs of adjacent equal sides; diagonals meet at 90°.
Worked examples

Sides 5, 5, 7 — all angles <90° → isosceles and acute.

Every square is also a rectangle (has four right angles).

Rhombus diagonals bisect at 90° (not equal); Rectangle diagonals are equal (not at 90°).

Topic 10 of 16 · Properties of shape
Finding Unknown Angles
Find unknown angles in triangles, quadrilaterals and regular polygons
Angle sum rules
Triangle
Angles always sum to 180°.
Quadrilateral
Angles always sum to 360°.
Any polygon
Sum = (n − 2) × 180°.
Regular polygon each angle
(n − 2) × 180° ÷ n.
Special triangle rules
Isosceles
Base angles are equal. Each = (180° − apex) ÷ 2.
Equilateral
All angles = 60°.
Exterior angle rule
An exterior angle = sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles.

Triangle: 180° · Quadrilateral: 360°

Polygon: (n−2)×180° · Regular each: ÷ n

Worked examples

Isosceles, apex 40° → base angles = (180−40)÷2 = 70° each.

Quadrilateral: 85°+110°+95°+x = 360° → x = 70°.

Exterior angle 115°, one interior 58° → other = 115−58 = 57°.

Topic 11 of 16 · Properties of shape
Circle Parts, Circumference and Area
Name and use circle parts: radius, diameter and circumference
Parts of a circle
Centre
Fixed middle point. All points on circle are equidistant from it.
Radius (r)
Distance from centre to any point on the circle.
Diameter (d)
Straight line through the centre. d = 2r.
Circumference
The perimeter — total distance around the circle.
Arc
Any part of the circumference.
Chord
Straight line joining two points on the circle, not through centre.
Sector
Pie-slice region: two radii + arc.
Tangent
Line touching the circle at exactly one point, at 90° to the radius.

Circumference: C = πd = 2πr

Area: A = πr²

π ≈ 3.14 or 22/7 (when r is a multiple of 7)

Worked examples

Radius 7 cm → C = 2 × 22/7 × 7 = 44 cm.

Diameter 10 cm → r = 5; A = 3.14 × 25 = 78.5 cm².

Area 28.26 cm² → r² = 28.26 ÷ 3.14 = 9 → r = 3 cm.

Topic 12 of 16 · Properties of shape
Vertically Opposite Angles
Use angle facts for vertically opposite angles
The rule
Definition
When two straight lines cross, four angles are formed. The two opposite pairs are always equal.
Adjacent angles
Next to each other at the intersection — always sum to 180°.
All four angles
Must sum to 360°.
Solving problems
Step 1
Identify vertically opposite angles — directly across the intersection.
Step 2
Set them equal to form an equation.
Step 3
Use the 180° rule for adjacent angles.
Step 4
Check: all four must sum to 360°.

Vertically opposite angles are equal

Adjacent angles at intersection sum to 180°

All four angles at intersection sum to 360°

Worked examples

One angle = 68° → opposite = 68°; adjacent = 112°; other adjacent = 112°.

3x = x + 60 (vertically opposite) → 2x = 60 → x = 30°.

Lines cross at 90° → all four angles are right angles (perpendicular lines).

Topic 13 of 16 · Position and direction
Reflections and Translations — Language
Reflect and translate shapes using the appropriate language
Reflection
Mirror line
The line the shape flips across (line of reflection).
Image
The reflected shape — same size, orientation reversed.
Describing it
State the mirror line equation, e.g. 'reflected in x = 2' or 'in y = 0'.
Preserved
Size, shape and area unchanged. Orientation reversed.
Translation
Definition
A slide — shape moves without rotating or reflecting.
Vector
Top number = x movement (+right, −left). Bottom = y movement (+up, −down).
Example
(3, −2) means 3 right and 2 down.
Preserved
Size, shape, area and orientation all unchanged.

Vector top = x movement, bottom = y movement

Reflection: orientation reverses

Translation: orientation preserved

Worked examples

Vector (−4, 3) → 4 left and 3 up.

'Reflected in y = 0' → reflected in the x-axis.

Translation: size, shape, area and orientation all preserved.

Topic 14 of 16 · Position and direction
Coordinates in All Four Quadrants
Describe positions on the full coordinate grid in all 4 quadrants
The four quadrants
Quadrant 1 (top right)
x positive, y positive. e.g. (3, 5)
Quadrant 2 (top left)
x negative, y positive. e.g. (−3, 5)
Quadrant 3 (bottom left)
x negative, y negative. e.g. (−3, −5)
Quadrant 4 (bottom right)
x positive, y negative. e.g. (3, −5)
Key skills
Format
Always write (x, y). Memory: 'along the corridor, then up or down the stairs.'
Plotting
Count along x-axis first, then up/down y-axis.
Midpoint
( (x₁+x₂)÷2 , (y₁+y₂)÷2 )

Always (x, y) — x first

Midpoint = ( (x₁+x₂)÷2 , (y₁+y₂)÷2 )

Worked examples

(−5, 3): x negative, y positive → Quadrant 2 (top left).

(3, −4): x positive, y negative → Quadrant 4 (bottom right).

Midpoint of (−2,6) and (4,−2): x=1, y=2 → (1, 2).

Drawing and Translating on a Grid
Draw and translate simple shapes on the coordinate plane
Plotting and drawing shapes
Plot each vertex
Mark every corner using its coordinates.
Join in order
Connect vertices in the order given. Close the shape.
Label
Label each vertex A, B, C …
Translating a shape
Apply to every vertex
Add x-part of vector to every x-coordinate. Add y-part to every y-coordinate.
Draw the image
Plot new vertices and join — image is identical in size and orientation.
Label image
Use A′, B′, C′ (A prime, B prime, C prime).
Find the vector
Vector = (x₂ − x₁, y₂ − y₁).

Apply vector to every vertex: (x+a, y+b)

Translation preserves size, shape and orientation

Label image as A′, B′, C′

Worked examples

A(1,1), B(4,1), C(1,5) + vector (3,2) → A′(4,3), B′(7,3), C′(4,7).

P moves from (−2,3) to (1,−1) → vector = (3, −4).

Area of image = area of original.

Topic 16 of 16 · Position and direction
Reflecting Shapes in the Axes
Reflect shapes in the axes
Reflecting in the x-axis (y = 0)
Rule
Keep x the same. Change the sign of y.
Example
(3, 4) → (3, −4) and (−2, −5) → (−2, 5)
Reflecting in the y-axis (x = 0)
Rule
Change the sign of x. Keep y the same.
Example
(3, 4) → (−3, 4) and (−1, −2) → (1, −2)
Reflecting in other lines
Line x = a
New x = 2a − old x. y stays the same.
Line y = b
New y = 2b − old y. x stays the same.
Distance check
Each point and image are the same perpendicular distance from the mirror line.

x-axis (y=0): negate y, keep x

y-axis (x=0): negate x, keep y

Line x=a: new x = 2a − old x

Worked examples

(4, −3) reflected in x-axis → (4, 3).

(−2, 5) reflected in y-axis → (2, 5).

(5, 3) reflected in line x=2 → new x = 2(2)−5 = −1 → (−1, 3).
