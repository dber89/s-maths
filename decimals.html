What is a Decimal?

Decimals are a way of writing numbers that aren't whole — they sit between whole numbers on the number line.

Place Value — the key idea

Every digit in a number has a place. The decimal point separates the whole part from the fractional part.

ThousandsHundredsTensUnits · TenthsHundredthsThousandths
347 . 268

So 347.268 means: 347 whole, then 2 tenths, 6 hundredths, and 8 thousandths.

How to read decimals out loud

  • 0.7 → "zero point seven" or "seven tenths"
  • 3.45 → "three point four five" (not "forty-five"!)
  • 0.09 → "zero point zero nine" or "nine hundredths"
  • 12.005 → "twelve point zero zero five" or "twelve and five thousandths"
11+ trick: Never say "twelve point nought nought five" — say each digit separately after the point. The examiners notice.

Trailing zeroes

Adding zeroes at the end of a decimal doesn't change its value.

0.5 = 0.50 = 0.500 — all equal to one half.

But 0.05 ≠ 0.5 — the position matters!

Exam watch: Questions often use this to catch you out when comparing decimals.

Comparing & Ordering Decimals

This is one of the trickiest decimal topics. Many students make errors here because they think the longer decimal is always bigger. It isn't.

The golden rule

Compare column by column from left to right — just like comparing whole numbers, but you might need to add placeholder zeroes first.

Is 0.35 bigger than 0.8?
Lots of students say "yes" because 35 > 8. Wrong! Compare column by column:
0.35 vs 0.8 — the tenths column says 3 < 8, so 0.35 < 0.8.

Method: line them up

To compare or order decimals, write them vertically aligned at the decimal point, padding with zeros where needed:

  Order: 0.45,  0.9,  0.405,  0.6

  0.450   ← added a zero
  0.900   ← added two zeros
  0.405
  0.600   ← added a zero

  Now compare: 0.405 < 0.45 < 0.6 < 0.9 ✓
Worked Example — 11+ Style

Question: Write these in ascending order: 1.7, 1.07, 1.701, 1.17

  • 1Write them aligned: 1.700 / 1.070 / 1.701 / 1.170
  • 2All start with 1 — look at tenths: 7, 0, 7, 1
  • 3Smallest tenths digit: 0 → that's 1.07
  • 4Next: tenths = 1 → that's 1.17
  • 5Remaining: 1.700 and 1.701 — same up to thousandths: 0 < 1 → 1.700 < 1.701
  • ✓Answer: 1.07, 1.17, 1.7, 1.701
⚡ Exam Tips
  • Always align the decimal points before comparing.
  • "Ascending" = smallest first; "descending" = largest first. Don't mix them up under pressure.
  • If two decimals look the same length, check every digit — even the last one matters.

Adding & Subtracting Decimals

Good news: you already know how to add and subtract whole numbers. Decimals use exactly the same method — with one critical rule.

The one rule: align the decimal point

Write numbers so the decimal points line up vertically. Then add or subtract exactly as you would with whole numbers — carrying and borrowing work the same way.

Always pad with zeros so every number has the same number of decimal places. This prevents silly column errors.
Worked Example — Addition

Calculate 12.6 + 4.73 + 0.008

  • 1Pad to 3 d.p.: 12.600 + 4.730 + 0.008
  • 2Add thousandths: 0+0+8 = 8
  • 3Add hundredths: 0+3+0 = 3
  • 4Add tenths: 6+7+0 = 13 → write 3, carry 1
  • 5Add units: 2+4+0+1(carried) = 7
  • 6Tens: 1+0 = 1
  • ✓Answer: 17.338
Worked Example — Subtraction

Calculate 5.2 − 1.847

  • 1Pad: 5.200 − 1.847
  • 2Thousandths: 0 − 7, can't do it. Borrow: 10 − 7 = 3
  • 3Hundredths: (0−1) after borrow → borrow again: 10−1−1 = 8? Careful here: 9−4−1(borrowed)= work column by column
  • 4Use standard column subtraction carefully with borrowing
  • ✓Answer: 3.353

Check: 3.353 + 1.847 = 5.200 ✓ — always check by adding back.

⚡ Exam Tips
  • Always check by doing the inverse operation (add back after subtracting).
  • Estimate first: 5.2 − 1.847 ≈ 5 − 2 = 3, so your answer should be near 3. If you get 33 or 0.3, you've made an error.
  • Money questions are decimals in disguise: £3.40 + £2.67 is just 3.40 + 2.67.

Multiplying & Dividing Decimals

The smartest approach: turn decimals into whole numbers, calculate, then correct the answer.

Multiplying decimals

Rule: Count the total decimal places in both numbers. Multiply ignoring the decimal point. Then put that many decimal places back into the answer.

Example: 3.4 × 2.5
  • 1Count d.p.: 3.4 has 1, 2.5 has 1 → total: 2 d.p.
  • 2Ignore points: 34 × 25 = 850
  • 3Put 2 d.p. back: 8.50 = 8.5
Example: 0.06 × 0.4
  • 1d.p.: 2 + 1 = 3 d.p.
  • 26 × 4 = 24
  • 33 d.p. in 24 → 0.024

Multiplying/dividing by powers of 10

This comes up constantly in 11+ papers:

OperationEffectExample
× 10Move digits 1 place LEFT3.47 × 10 = 34.7
× 100Move digits 2 places LEFT3.47 × 100 = 347
× 1000Move digits 3 places LEFT3.47 × 1000 = 3470
÷ 10Move digits 1 place RIGHT56 ÷ 10 = 5.6
÷ 100Move digits 2 places RIGHT56 ÷ 100 = 0.56
÷ 1000Move digits 3 places RIGHT56 ÷ 1000 = 0.056
Think of it as: the digits stay still, but the decimal point moves. Or equivalently, the decimal point stays still and all the digits shift — either visualisation works.

Dividing decimals

Make the divisor a whole number by multiplying both numbers by the same power of 10.

Example: 4.8 ÷ 0.6
  • 10.6 has 1 d.p. → multiply both by 10
  • 248 ÷ 6 = 8
Example: 0.072 ÷ 0.9
  • 1Multiply both by 10: 0.72 ÷ 9
  • 20.72 ÷ 9 = 0.08

Fraction → Decimal

Method: divide the top by the bottom.

Example: Convert 7/8 to a decimal
  • 17 ÷ 8 — set up long division
  • 27.000 ÷ 8: 8 goes into 70 eight times (64), remainder 6
  • 360 ÷ 8 = 7 r 4; 40 ÷ 8 = 5
  • ✓7/8 = 0.875

Alternatively: 7/8 = 7 × (1/8) = 7 × 0.125 = 0.875 ✓ (faster if you know 1/8)

Decimal → Fraction

Example: Convert 0.36 to a fraction
  • 10.36 = 36/100 (two decimal places → denominator 100)
  • 2Simplify: HCF of 36 and 100 is 4
  • ✓0.36 = 9/25
Pattern: 1 d.p. → denominator 10 · 2 d.p. → denominator 100 · 3 d.p. → denominator 1000. Then always simplify.

Rounding Decimals

Rounding is tested constantly in 11+ papers, both directly and as part of estimation questions.

The rounding rule

  1. Find the digit in the place you're rounding to.
  2. Look at the next digit to the right.
  3. If it's 5 or more, round up. If it's 4 or less, round down (keep the digit the same).
Memory hook: "Five and above, give it a shove. Four and below, let it go."

⚡ Exam Tips
  • Estimation questions: round each number to 1 SF first, then calculate. This is faster and less error-prone.
  • If the answer to a word problem seems unreasonably large or small, check your rounding.
  • Read the question carefully — "to the nearest tenth" means 1 d.p.; "to the nearest hundredth" means 2 d.p.
